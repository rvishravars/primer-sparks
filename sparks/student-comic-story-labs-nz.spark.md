# Student Comic Story Labs (NZ)

# 1. Spark Narrative

**The Observation:** In the everyday lives of many New Zealand primary and intermediate students, digital experiences take up a growing share of attention: watching videos, scrolling feeds, or playing pre-made games. Schools already invest heavily in reading, writing, and creativity, but the pull of these passive digital habits means older forms of self-directed creative play like drawing, making comics, and trading stories in person can get crowded out. Classroom writing time is full of important learning goals, yet it can still be hard to give every child a playful, visual way to turn their ideas into shared stories.

At the same time:
- Many classrooms now have regular access to devices (Chromebooks, tablets) and decent internet.
- Generative AI can produce safe, child-friendly images on demand.
- Teachers are asked to lift literacy, creativity, and digital citizenship within finite classroom time and tools.

Traditional comics publishing cannot react at classroom speed:
- Print comics are slow and expensive to produce.
- They rarely feature the local worlds, cultures, and in-jokes that matter to Kiwi kids.
- Industry tends to follow global trends, not classroom-level creativity.

**The Shift:** Instead of waiting for publishers, imagine that **classrooms themselves become small, safe comic studios**:
- Children (ages ~6–12) describe scenes, characters, and dialogue in their own words or voice.
- A school-managed platform uses AI only to turn those ideas into child-friendly panels and to suggest panel layouts.
- Teachers set short, purposeful comic challenges tied to literacy goals (e.g., "show, don’t tell", point of view, endings).
- Students share finished comics with their class, syndicate them to buddy classes, and optionally print simple zines for the school library.

The new pattern in a school term could look like:
- Weekly or fortnightly: 30–45 minute "comic lab" sessions in literacy blocks.
- Term projects: class anthologies on shared themes (Matariki, local environment, history, te ao Māori stories where appropriate and guided).
- Cross-class exchanges: comics swapped between schools or regions.

This is **not** another social network. There are no public follower graphs or open feeds. Instead, teachers curate who can read which comics, and simple readership stats (for example, how many classmates read a comic or finished a chapter) can be used for light-touch prizes, reading challenges, and school competitions.

Children get:
- A playful, visual way to practice storytelling, sequencing, and dialogue.
- A sense of pride and authorship when their comics are read and discussed by peers, with friendly recognition based on readership and effort rather than likes or popularity contests.

Teachers get:
- A structured digital activity that is **still fundamentally about writing and story craft**.
- A way to show progress in narrative skills across the year.

Schools and libraries get:
- A stream of locally-created, culturally relevant comics that can be printed or displayed.

The spark: **Turn New Zealand classrooms into "Student Comic Story Labs" where kids write the stories and AI only helps with panels and layouts, reviving comics as a shared, school-led culture while building storytelling skills and positive peer communities.**

---

# 2. Hypothesis Formalization

**Hypothesis Statement**
> "If New Zealand primary and intermediate schools adopt a simple, school-moderated digital platform where students (ages ~6–12) write their own short comics and use AI only for child-friendly images and layout suggestions, then:
> - Students in participating classes will show **measurably stronger narrative writing skills** (structure, character, dialogue, and visual sequencing) compared to similar classes not using the platform.
> - Participating classes will report **higher levels of positive peer feedback and story-sharing** around student work.
> - At least **half of participating schools** will regularly produce and display **student-made comic collections** (digital or printed) by the end of the first school year."

In plain language:
- We expect this platform to make kids **better at telling stories**, not just better at clicking through apps.
- We expect classrooms to become **friendlier story circles**, with kids reading and responding to each other’s work.
- We expect a visible **return of comics as a school-wide medium**: boards, corridors, digital displays, and small zines.

**Null Hypothesis**
> "Using a school-moderated AI comic platform does **not** lead to clearly better narrative writing outcomes, does **not** meaningfully improve peer story-sharing or feedback culture, and does **not** result in a noticeable, sustained increase in student-made comics being displayed or collected in participating schools."

# 3. Simulation / Modeling Plan

## Model Type
- Start with a **simple cohort model** at the classroom and school level, before running any real pilots.
- Then move to **real classroom pilots** in a small number of New Zealand schools (e.g., a mix of deciles and regions) and compare with matched classes not using the platform.

## Inputs

**Classroom and school context:**
- Year levels involved (e.g., Years 3–8).
- Device availability (1:1, small sets of tablets, shared computer lab).
- Frequency and duration of comic sessions (e.g., weekly 40 minutes vs fortnightly).

**Student engagement variables:**
- Number of students who create at least one comic per term.
- Average number of panels per comic and number of drafts.
- Number of peer comments or "kind feedback" actions per student.

**Story quality and progression:**
- Teacher or rubric-based scores on:
  - Beginning–middle–end structure.
  - Clarity of sequence across panels.
  - Use of dialogue and captions.
  - Character motivation and change.
- Simple pre/post measures using short narrative writing tasks (not only comics).

**Platform and AI behavior:**
- How strictly AI is limited to images and layout (no full-script generation).
- Child-safety filters and blocked content categories.
- Latency and reliability on typical New Zealand school networks.

**Comics culture indicators:**
- Number of class or school anthologies produced per term.
- Number of printed zines or displays in library / corridors.
- Participation in inter-class or inter-school comic exchanges.

## Expected Outputs

From the model and pilots, we want to estimate:
- The relationship between **time spent in comic labs** and improvements in narrative writing scores.
- The **distribution of participation** (do a few keen students dominate, or is it broad-based?).
- How quickly **a visible comics culture** can emerge in a school (e.g., by the end of Term 2 vs Term 4).
- Likely teacher workload impact:
  - Time spent setting up challenges.
  - Time spent moderating and giving feedback.
- Infrastructure requirements that are realistic for typical New Zealand schools.

## Sensitivity Analysis

We can vary and test in the model and pilots:
- Session frequency (weekly vs fortnightly vs "intensive weeks").
- Device access patterns (1:1 Chromebooks vs a shared set of tablets rotated through groups).
- Different moderation approaches:
  - Full teacher pre-approval of published comics.
  - Post-publication review with strong filters.
- Different emphasis in teacher prompts:
  - Comics as literacy/English tool.
  - Comics tied to other curriculum areas (history, science, te reo Māori).

We watch how these affect:
- Gains in narrative writing scores.
- Equity of participation (by gender, background, year level).
- Teacher-reported sustainability and workload.
- The richness and diversity of displayed student comics.

# 4. Evaluation Strategy

**Study Design:**
- Use a **clustered design** at the class or school level.
- Group A: Classes using the Student Comic Story Labs platform.
- Group B: Similar classes (year level, region, decile) continuing with their usual literacy activities without the platform.

**Primary metrics:**
- **Narrative writing improvement:**
  - Pre/post moderation of short narrative tasks (blind marking, shared rubric) across both groups.
  - Specific attention to sequencing, dialogue, and character development.
- **Peer community indicators:**
  - Number and quality of peer comments per student (e.g., "I liked…", "This made me think…") in Group A.
  - Student survey items about feeling proud of their work, being read by peers, and giving feedback.
- **Comics culture revival:**
  - Count and describe visible comic artefacts: printed zines, wall displays, digital showcases.
  - Teacher and librarian reports on how often students pick up and discuss peer-made comics.

**Secondary metrics:**
- Teacher workload and perceived value (short surveys and interviews).
- Student attitudes toward writing and reading before vs after.

**Data sources:**
- Platform logs: number of comics created, edited, shared; peer feedback events.
- Rubric-based writing assessments (non-platform tasks).
- Photographic or digital records of displays and zines.
- Short, age-appropriate student surveys in participating classes.

**Success criteria (example thresholds):**
- Group A shows **meaningful gains** (for example, half a rubric level or more) in narrative structure and sequencing compared to Group B over two terms.
- A large majority of students in Group A report that they enjoy sharing their stories and reading others’ comics.
- At least **50% of participating schools** produce termly comics displays or collections.

# 5. Feedback & Critique

## Internal Critique

**Hidden assumptions:**
- Students are willing and motivated to write text or dictate stories, not just click through image options.
- Teachers can find room within their existing literacy and digital programmes to run comic sessions.
- AI image filters are reliable enough to keep content child-safe without constant human review.
- The novelty of AI-assisted comics does not wear off too quickly.

**Weaknesses:**
- Risk that some students focus mainly on visuals, with minimal attention to text quality, unless the platform and teacher prompts are carefully designed.
- There is a chance this feels like "one more thing" unless it is clearly woven into existing literacy goals and resources.
- Schools with limited devices or bandwidth may not be able to participate fully.

**Equity and cultural considerations:**
- Need to respect and properly support local stories, including Māori and Pacific narratives, without trivialising or misrepresenting them.
- Careful guidance is needed so that AI-generated art styles and prompts do not erase local visual traditions.

## Counter-Hypothesis

> "Most of the improvement in storytelling and classroom community could be achieved with simple, low-tech tools (paper comics, traditional writing workshops, peer critique circles), and the AI comic platform adds complexity and distraction without meaningfully better outcomes. Any observed gains may come mainly from teachers dedicating more time and structure to storytelling, not from AI-assisted images or layouts."

# 7. Revision Notes

As data and feedback come in, we might revise:

- **Target age bands:**
  - If Years 5–8 show the strongest gains, we may focus more on upper primary/intermediate.
- **Feature set:**
  - Tighten or relax AI layout help depending on whether it supports or undermines student planning skills.
  - Adjust how strongly the platform nudges students to revise text versus adding more panels.
- **Teacher tools:**
  - Add better class dashboards, exemplars, and curriculum-aligned prompts if teachers report friction.
- **Cultural framing:**
  - Work with local educators and communities to better integrate te ao Māori and local stories in a respectful way.

New maturity level could move from **conceptual spark** to **pilot-ready program** once:
- A minimal platform prototype exists.
- Clear teacher guides and example lesson plans are drafted.
- A small set of New Zealand schools agree to run structured pilots.
